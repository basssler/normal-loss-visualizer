Lead-Time Demand Distribution

The red shaded right tail represents the probability of stockout only. The normal loss function NL(z) is not this shaded area — it measures average overshoot beyond the reorder point in standardized units.

What Is the Normal Loss Function?

NL(z) is not the probability of a stockout. It measures the expected size of the overshoot beyond the reorder point R, in standardized units. Three scenarios below show why shortage size varies even within the tail.

Left of R: no stockout → shortage = 0
Just right of R: stockout exists but overshoot is small
Far right of R: stockout exists and overshoot is large
Key: the red tail shows probability; NL(z) captures weighted average overshoot across all those right-tail outcomes.

Computed Outputs

Read in order: safety stock shifts R right → red tail gives stockout probability → NL(z) measures standardized overshoot → nR = σ × NL(z) gives units short.

Derivation

Show the math trail
Step 01
Convert reorder point to z-score
z = (R − μX) / σX
Step 02
Evaluate the normal loss function
NL(z) = f(z) − z [1 − F(z)]
Step 03
Convert standardized overshoot to actual units
nR = σX · NL(z)
f(z) = (1/√2π) · exp(−z²/2)
F(z) = ∫−∞z f(t) dt
